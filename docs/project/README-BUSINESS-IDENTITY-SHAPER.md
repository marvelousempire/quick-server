# Business Identity Shaper Framework

## What is the Business Identity Shaper?

The **Business Identity Shaper** is a comprehensive framework/system used to build business identity sites like the LearnMappers site. It's a reusable product that provides all the logic, structure, and nuances needed to create a complete business identity platform.

## Core Purpose: RAG-Shaping for Entities

**The fundamental purpose of the Business Identity Shaper is to shape entities (Resources, Services, Persons, Companies) for RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) systems.**

This means every entity in the system is structured to be:
- **Fully Encompassing** — Complete, comprehensive data with all relevant attributes, relationships, and context
- **Immediately Learnable** — Clear, accessible, and understandable at a glance without additional context

Each entity contains rich, structured data that allows an AI system (or human) to instantly understand:
- **What it is** — Clear identification and categorization
- **What it does** — Purpose, outcomes, capabilities, and abilities
- **How it's used** — Use cases, scenarios, and applications
- **What it produces** — Outcomes, deliverables, and results
- **How it relates** — Relationships to other entities (Resources ↔ Services, etc.)
- **Complete context** — All metadata, specifications, and relevant information

This RAG-ready structure ensures that entities can be:
- **Easily retrieved** by semantic search and embedding-based queries
- **Immediately understood** without requiring additional context
- **Effectively used** in generation tasks with complete information
- **Properly related** to other entities through clear relationship mappings

The Business Identity Shaper doesn't just organize business data—it shapes it into a form that is optimized for AI understanding and retrieval, making every entity immediately learnable and fully encompassing.

## The Fundamental Insight

To truly build out the Business Identity Shaper, we needed to understand the relationship between two core components:

### Resources & Services

**Resources** are what you have or have access to:
- **Tools** — Drills, saws, wrenches, etc.
- **Equipment** — 3D printers, scanners, power systems, etc.
- **Appliances** — Refrigerators, washers, dryers, ovens, dishwashers, etc.
- **Materials** — Hardware, consumables, supplies, etc.
- **People/Contacts** — Team members, partners, contractors, specialists, etc.

**Services** are what you can do/provide using those resources (e.g., furniture assembly, TV mounting, electrical work, home repairs).

### The Relationship

- **Resources enable services** — You need the drill to provide drilling services; you need a washer/dryer to provide laundry services; you need an electrician contact to provide electrical services
- **Services require resources** — To offer furniture assembly, you need the right tools; to offer laundry services, you need appliances; to offer electrical work, you need an electrician (person resource)
- **You need both** — Understanding what resources you have (tools, equipment, appliances, materials, people) AND what services you can provide with them is essential

### Why This Matters

This insight was crucial for building the framework because:

1. **You can't provide services without resources** — If you don't have a drill, you can't offer drilling services; if you don't have a washer/dryer, you can't offer laundry services; if you don't have an electrician contact, you can't offer electrical services
2. **Resources without services are underutilized** — Having a drill but not offering drilling services means missed opportunities; having appliances but not offering appliance-related services means missed opportunities; having a skilled contractor contact but not offering their services means missed opportunities
3. **The relationship defines capability** — Your business identity is shaped by what you can do (services) with what you have (resources: tools, equipment, appliances, materials, AND people)

### Examples

*Tool Resource:*
- **Resource**: "Compact 1/2" drill driver" (Battery 20V) - Tool
- **Service**: "Furniture Assembly" (requires drill for mounting hardware, drilling pilot holes, etc.)
- **Relationship**: The drill enables the furniture assembly service

*Appliance Resource:*
- **Resource**: "Washer/Dryer Combo" (Energy-efficient, large capacity) - Appliance
- **Service**: "Laundry Services" (requires washer/dryer for cleaning and drying clothes)
- **Relationship**: The washer/dryer enables the laundry services

*Person Resource:*
- **Resource**: "Electrician Contact" (John, licensed electrician) - Person
- **Service**: "Electrical Work" (requires electrician for wiring, panel upgrades, etc.)
- **Relationship**: The electrician contact enables the electrical services

This relationship mapping is what makes the Business Identity Shaper framework powerful — it connects what you have (tools, equipment, appliances, materials, people) to what you can do, creating a complete picture of your business capabilities.

## Framework Components

The Business Identity Shaper includes:

### 1. Resources (What You Have)

Resources are the tools, equipment, appliances, materials, people, and assets you own or have access to:
- **Tools** — Drills, saws, wrenches, etc.
- **Equipment** — 3D printers, scanners, power systems, etc.
- **Appliances** — Refrigerators, washers, dryers, ovens, dishwashers, etc.
- **Materials** — Hardware, consumables, supplies, etc.
- **People/Contacts** — Team members, partners, contractors, specialists, etc.

**Each resource has** — These key factors that help identify and evaluate each resource:
- **Purpose** — Why this resource exists and what value it provides
- **Use Cases** — Specific scenarios where this resource is applied or used
- **Outcomes** — What happens when this resource is applied/used (results, achievements, deliverables)
- **Capabilities** — What this resource can do (core capabilities and functions)
- **Abilities** — Specific abilities, skills, or features this resource possesses
- **Additional info** — Specifications, condition, location, maintenance (for tools/equipment) or skills, expertise, availability (for people)

**Examples**: 
- A "Compact 1/2" drill driver" (Tool) — Purpose: drilling holes and driving screws; Use Cases: furniture assembly, mounting hardware; Outcomes: secure installations, professional finish; Capabilities: variable speed control, torque adjustment; Abilities: drill through wood/metal, drive screws, countersink
- A "Washer/Dryer Combo" (Appliance) — Purpose: clean and dry laundry efficiently; Use Cases: residential laundry, commercial cleaning; Outcomes: clean clothes, time savings, energy efficiency; Capabilities: multiple wash cycles, temperature control, spin drying; Abilities: large capacity, energy-efficient, smart controls
- A "Contractor" (Person) — Purpose: provide electrical expertise; Use Cases: wiring projects, panel upgrades; Outcomes: code-compliant installations, safe electrical systems; Capabilities: electrical design, installation, troubleshooting; Abilities: licensed electrician, 20+ years experience, specializes in residential

### 2. Services (What You Can Do)

Services are the offerings you provide using your resources:
- **Service offerings** — Furniture assembly, TV mounting, system setup, etc.
- **Each service has** — Purpose, use cases, outcomes, required resources, capabilities

**Example**: "Furniture Assembly" service that uses the drill (and other tools) to assemble furniture.

### 3. The Relationship (Resources → Services)

The framework maps the connection between resources and services:
- **Which resources enable which services** — The drill enables furniture assembly; the washer/dryer enables laundry services; the electrician contact enables electrical services
- **Which services require which resources** — Furniture assembly requires the drill (and other tools); laundry services require appliances; electrical work requires an electrician (person resource)
- **Capability projection** — See what you can accomplish with available resources (tools, equipment, appliances, materials, AND people)
- **Gap analysis** — Identify what resources you need for desired services (tools, appliances, materials, OR people)

**Examples**: 
- The relationship shows that your drill resource enables your furniture assembly service
- The relationship shows that your electrician contact (person resource) enables your electrical services

### 4. Schema-Based Data Structures (RAG-Optimized)

All schemas are designed to be RAG-ready, with fields that make entities immediately learnable:

- **Service Schema** — Define services with purpose, use cases, outcomes, capabilities, plus:
  - **RAG Context Fields** — Semantic context, learnable summary, embedding text, query patterns
  - **Relationship Mappings** — Clear connections to required resources
  - **Complete Process Documentation** — Step-by-step procedures for full understanding

- **Resource Schema** — Catalog tools, equipment, appliances, materials, AND people/contacts with:
  - **Serial Numbers** — Unique identifiers for products/resources to prevent duplicates
  - **Core Identification Factors** — Purpose, Use Cases, Outcomes, Capabilities, Abilities
  - **RAG Context Fields** — Semantic context, learnable summary, embedding text, retrieval tags
  - **Specifications & Metadata** — Complete technical and contextual information
  - **Relationship Data** — What services this resource enables, what it requires

- **Person Schema** — Track people as resources (contacts, team members, partners, contractors) with:
  - **UDIN (Unique Digital Identifier Number)** — Unique identifier for contacts to prevent duplicates
  - **Core Identification Factors** — Purpose, Use Cases, Outcomes, Capabilities, Abilities
  - **RAG Context Fields** — Semantic context, learnable summary, embedding text, query patterns
  - **Contact & Relationship Info** — Complete contact details and relationship context
  - **History & Examples** — Real-world interaction examples for context

- **Company Schema** — Manage partners and companies (also resources) with:
  - **RAG Context Fields** — Semantic context, learnable summary, embedding text
  - **Relationship Mappings** — Clear business relationship context
  - **Service/Product Catalog** — What the company provides
  - **Partnership Context** — How the relationship works

- **Relationship Schema** — Map connections between entities with:
  - **RAG Context Fields** — Semantic context explaining the relationship
  - **Strength & Direction** — Relationship importance and flow
  - **Examples** — Real-world examples of the relationship in action
  - **Knowledge Graph Data** — Graph representation for relationship queries

**RAG Context Fields** (added to all entities):
- `rag.semanticContext` — Rich, comprehensive text optimized for semantic embeddings
- `rag.learnableSummary` — Immediately understandable summary (self-contained)
- `rag.keyConcepts` — Key terms and concepts for retrieval
- `rag.queryPatterns` — Common questions this entity answers
- `rag.embeddingText` — Optimized text for embedding generation
- `rag.retrievalTags` — Tags optimized for search and retrieval
- `rag.contextualExamples` — Real-world examples demonstrating the entity
- `rag.knowledgeGraph` — Graph representation for relationship queries

**Key Insight**: All resources (tools, equipment, appliances, materials, people) share the same core identification factors: Purpose, Use Cases, Outcomes, Capabilities, and Abilities. These factors help identify which resources to use in which cases, and are enhanced with RAG context fields for immediate learnability.

**Duplicate Prevention**: Serial numbers (for resources) and UDINs (for contacts) ensure no accidental duplicates are created. If you have two of the same tool, they should have different serial numbers. If you have two contacts with the same name, they should have different UDINs.

## How It Works

The framework uses a comprehensive schema-based approach that starts with the fundamental Resources ↔ Services relationship:

1. **Catalog Resources** — Document all tools, equipment, appliances, materials, AND people/contacts you have (what you own or have access to)
2. **Define Services** — Document all services you can provide (what you can do)
3. **Map Relationships** — Connect resources to services (which tools, equipment, appliances, OR people enable which services)
4. **Identify Capabilities** — See what you can accomplish with your available resources (tools, equipment, appliances, materials, people)
5. **Discover Gaps** — Identify what resources you need for desired services (tools, appliances, materials, OR people)
6. **Optimize Offerings** — Focus on services where you have the right resources (tools, equipment, appliances, people) and highest demand
7. **Analyze Patterns** — Understand where your highest value and demand areas are

### The Workflow

```
Resources (What You Have)
    ↓
    Enables
    ↓
Services (What You Can Do)
    ↓
    Requires
    ↓
Resources (What You Need)
```

**Example Flows:**

*Tool Resource:*
1. You have a drill (Tool Resource)
2. The drill enables furniture assembly service (Service)
3. Furniture assembly requires the drill (Relationship)
4. You can offer furniture assembly because you have the drill (Capability)
5. If you want to offer TV mounting, you might need a stud finder (Gap Analysis)

*Appliance Resource:*
1. You have a washer/dryer (Appliance Resource)
2. The washer/dryer enables laundry services (Service)
3. Laundry services require the washer/dryer (Relationship)
4. You can offer laundry services because you have the washer/dryer (Capability)
5. If you want to offer commercial laundry, you might need larger capacity appliances (Gap Analysis)

*Person Resource:*
1. You have an electrician contact (Person Resource)
2. The electrician enables electrical services (Service)
3. Electrical work requires an electrician (Relationship)
4. You can offer electrical services because you have the electrician contact (Capability)
5. If you want to offer plumbing services, you might need a plumber contact (Gap Analysis)
